Master's Research Project

Microfluidics — Effects of Mechanical Stress on Metastatic Phenotype

Imperial College London · 2025–Present

Context

Circulating tumour cells experience mechanical stress during capillary transit. This project investigated whether that stress causes breast cancer cells to shift toward a more metastatic phenotype.

My Role

Individual research project. I managed all fabrication, experiments, and analysis, with training from my supervisor.

Approach

I fabricated microfluidic capillary bifurcations in PDMS, then conducted perfusion experiments to expose breast cancer cells to capillary-like mechanical stresses. I extracted mRNA, synthesised cDNA, and ran qPCRs on a gene panel I identified via literature review. I also conducted a 24-hour proliferation assay to assess the effects of capillary transit over a longer time frame.

Outcome

Found trends suggesting altered inflammation and chemotaxis responses to capillary stresses. Gained hands-on experience across the full workflow — from microfluidic fabrication through to cell culture maintenance, molecular biology and data interpretation.

Third Year Group Project

Microfluidics — Effects of Shear Rate on Cell Viability

Imperial College London · 2024–2025

Context

Syringe-based cell therapy delivery kills up to 95% of cells due to high fluid shear rates. This project aimed to measure the critical shear rate threshold to inform better delivery device design.

My Role

Seven-person group project. I independently led all device fabrication and rapid prototyping, and took charge of COMSOL simulations. I contributed to MATLAB modelling in a subgroup of three.

Approach

Used COMSOL to determine entrance/exit lengths for flow stabilisation. Used MATLAB to model velocity and shear rate profiles. Designed and fabricated eight prototypes iteratively — widened the inlet funnel to prevent cell overshoot, tested winding and gradual-expansion outlets, and found that a sudden expansion slowed and spread cells for more accurate imaging.

Outcome

Delivered a working device geometry that controlled cell positioning and enabled clear imaging. Learned that fast iteration and direct observation beat theoretical assumptions of geometry.

Third Year Coursework

Finite Element Analysis of a Clevis

Imperial College London · 2025

Context

A clevis joint for a prosthetic test rig was failing under 1.5kN tensile loading. The original design had a safety factor of 0.16 and fatigue life of approximately 100 cycles. The task was to redesign the geometry to meet static and fatigue requirements while meeting clearance constraints.

My Role

Individual coursework. Full ownership of analysis, redesign, and validation.

Approach

Ran a mesh convergence study, then conducted static and fatigue FEA in SolidWorks. Identified that stress concentrations at sharp internal corners were driving both static failure and fatigue crack initiation. Iterated through geometric modifications — filleting high-stress regions, adding reinforcement, adjusting wall thicknesses — while checking each iteration against clearance constraints.

Outcome

Increased the safety factor from 0.16 to 2.62 and fatigue life from ~100 cycles to over 1 million. Learned how sensitive stress distribution is to small geometric features (fillets, radii), and how to balance competing constraints like strength, clearance, and weight.

Second Year Group Project

A Physiotherapy Tool for Eye Muscles

Imperial College London · 2023–2024

Context

Convergence insufficiency affects 1 in 20 people and is treated with repetitive eye exercises. Patient compliance is poor, especially in children. The goal was to develop a VR tool that gamifies these exercises to improve engagement.

My Role

I co-led a team of ten and co-developed the game mechanics in Unity as part of a 2-person subteam. I also proposed the final gamification concept the team adopted.

Approach

Structured the project by breaking work into sections and assigning tasks based on individual strengths. Reached out to an ophthalmologist to validate clinical feasibility. Produced a product specification document covering features, requirements, and risks. Took inspiration from existing games and merged them with physiotherapy exercises to produce engaging game mechanics.

Outcome

Produced a functional prototype of a VR physiotherapy tool aimed at children. Learned how clinical input early in the design process prevents wasted effort — the ophthalmologist's feedback shaped our direction before we committed to building.
